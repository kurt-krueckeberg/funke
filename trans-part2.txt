## 2. The Sources of Livelihood of the Einlieger

The manually laboring classes in the Principality of Lippe, among whom,
besides the Einlieger (*resident landless tenants/lodgers*), are also
counted the small landowners who live under the same conditions, support
themselves chiefly through the linen industry and then through brickmaking
work abroad.

### a.

In the already cited work *On the Condition of the Heuerleute, etc.*, we
expressed our views on the decline of the linen industry in general. What
we said there also applies to Lippe. At present, even with all possible
diligence, a manually laboring family can no longer secure its livelihood
solely through domestic industry in flax or hemp, in view of the spread of
machine spinning and machine weaving. Spinning and weaving are now to be
regarded only as secondary work.

On the whole, however, the linen industry in Lippe was formerly less
important than in Ravensberg, Minden, Osnabrück, and so forth. For that
reason, the decline of the German linen trade has not had such thoroughly
harmful consequences there as in the regions just named, where the landless
population was not infrequently wholly dependent on earnings from spinning
and weaving, and where, under changed circumstances, no work replacing this
domestic industry has yet been found, and probably will not be found.

England, as is well known, has excluded us almost everywhere from the linen
trade, so that finally only Cuba remained for the sale of our products; and
meanwhile England has already forced its way into that market as well. With
the ever-expanding production of cotton in America, it has also come about
that even in the West Indies cotton fabrics have partly displaced linen
fabrics.

This, however, can always be the case only to a certain degree, since for
tropical countries there is no more suitable clothing than linen, which is
very durable and at the same time cooling. It is therefore certainly to be
expected that, despite the machines, a more favorable time will come for
the domestic linen industry once the political conditions of tropical
America have become more settled, and provided we do not neglect to enter
into favorable direct trade relations with it. On the one hand, hand-spun
linen, because of its greater strength and durability as well as its other
advantages, will then hold its own alongside machine linen; on the other
hand, the industry of the landless population will turn toward more careful
preparation of flax for the machines.

According to information from a Kolon (*hereditary farmholder*), in Lippe
about twenty to thirty years ago twelve pieces of so-called Kaufgarn (*yarn
spun for sale*) were supplied for one Taler. At present, fifteen to
eighteen pieces are given for one Taler. It has been claimed that the
difference in earnings cannot be quite so great, since nowadays spinning is
generally done with two hands, which was not formerly the case. It must be
observed, however, that although with two hands perhaps four pieces of yarn
are now spun instead of the former two or two and a half, this yarn is of
poorer quality; indeed, one has even had to supply thirty-six pieces for
one Taler. At the same time, spinning with two hands of course requires
twice as much flax.

If this flax is bought, one receives seven pounds uncleaned for one Taler,
of which about four to five pounds remain after cleaning; from these the
spinner can, with the greatest difficulty, spin only thirty-six pieces.
Thus the work would have to be done entirely for nothing if only one Taler
is paid for thirty-six pieces.

Meanwhile, even when yarn prices are higher, the earnings are so small that
a family dependent on spinning absolutely cannot subsist. Usually the flax
is not bought, but is grown by the Einlieger and small landowners
themselves. In that case, however, they must rent land suitable for flax
cultivation, buy the flaxseed, and, at a time when manual labor is well
paid, use their labor power for preparing the flax. There is, to be sure,
still some profit when the flax is grown by the family itself, since their
own labor is valued less highly than it would otherwise be paid. Yet there
are also many regions where flax does not thrive especially well, or does
not thrive at all, so that the only remaining option is to buy it raw,
assuming one wishes to occupy oneself with spinning at all during spare
hours.

Not infrequently the larger landowners grow flax for their Einlieger on
their fields at a low price. Likewise, servants among the larger landowners
have some flax grown for them. As regards the preparation of flax, much
remains to be desired; however, more care has recently been devoted to
spinning. If spinning is done with two hands, however, only very coarse
yarn can be supplied.

### b.

A very distinctive source of livelihood for the manually laboring class in
Lippe is brickmaking work abroad, or the so-called Hollandsgehen (*seasonal
labor migration to Holland or abroad*). Just as here in northern Osnabrück
the Torfbaggerer (*peat diggers*) who go to Holstein, Schleswig,
Brandenburg, East and West Prussia, and Posen are called Dänemarksgänger
(*Denmark-goers*), because they originally went first to Holstein and
Schleswig, so in Lippe all brickworkers abroad are called Hollandsgänger
(*Holland-goers*) or Friesländer (*Friesland-goers*), because they first
went to Holland and Friesland, although a very large part of them now
migrate temporarily in an entirely opposite direction. Lippers also go as
Torfbaggerer to West Friesland and Groningen, and less often as manual
laborers of other kinds, for example as grass mowers, gardeners, masons,
carpenters, glue boilers, and so forth, as is the case in the Principality
of Osnabrück.
